🤝 Pickleball Etiquette

Play with respect & sportsmanship

Line Calls
📣
Only Call a Ball Out If You're 100% Sure
▼
The ball must touch the ground completely outside the line — if any part of the ball touches the line, it's in. If you're 99% sure, give your opponent the benefit of the doubt. Making close calls won't win you many games anyway — you win by being better, not by making questionable calls.
🤝
Overrule Your Partner If They Got It Wrong
▼
If your partner calls a ball out and you think it was in, speak up and overrule them — say "I think that was in, let's give them the point." It might create a little friction with your partner, but if they're mature they'll respect it. Honest play matters more than keeping the peace with your own team.
🚫
Don't Argue With Opponents Over Their Calls
▼
If your opponents make a call you disagree with, generally don't argue or challenge them — especially in practice or recreational games. Raising tensions over a call isn't worth it. Keep things civil on the court. If you feel it's a pattern, you can calmly note it once, then let it go.
Self-Officiating
🙋
Always Call Rules on Yourself
▼
If you break a rule and no one calls it, call it on yourself. Common examples:
  • Stepping in the kitchen on a volley
  • Foot fault on your serve
  • Ball bouncing twice before you hit it
Your opponents may have seen it but didn't want to say anything. Calling it yourself builds trust and shows you're playing fair. Never try to win by breaking the rules.
Serving
🔢
Call the Score Loudly — Then Wait for Everyone
▼
Before every serve: announce the score loudly so all four players hear it, then wait until your opponents are looking at you and in the ready position before you serve. Serving before they're set is called a "quick serve" and gives you an unfair advantage. The sequence is: call the score → confirm everyone's ready → serve.
Sportsmanship
😤
Don't Be a Sore Winner or a Sore Loser
▼
Sore winner: being obnoxiously loud, over-celebrating every point, making your opponents feel bad for losing. Even in a rec game, don't go overboard.

Sore loser: making excuses when you lose — "that was a lucky shot," "I wasn't trying my hardest." Don't justify why they beat you. If anything, compliment them. Just try to have fun whether you win or lose.
🏅
Never Get Mad at Your Partner
▼
When your partner misses, they already feel bad — they're not out there trying to lose. Don't yell, sigh, or show frustration. The best things you can say: "Next point," "Don't worry about it," "Good try." You don't even need to say anything — just stay positive. A demoralized partner loses more points, not fewer.
💬
No Unsolicited Advice
▼
Don't coach your partner or your opponents unless they specifically ask. Constant tips during a game feel like pressure, not help — the other person may feel like they're playing badly because of all your feedback. Keep the mental load low. Save the coaching for after the game if they want it.
🎯
Don't Target the Weakest Player in Rec Games
▼
In a tournament, targeting the weaker player is smart strategy. In a rec game, it's annoying and not fun for anyone. The stronger player on the other team isn't going to touch the ball, and the weaker one gets discouraged. Instead, treat it as a practice game — work on your third shot drop, your dinking, your hands — and keep hitting to both players to keep the game fun for everyone.
Court Awareness
🚶
Never Walk Onto or Through a Court Mid-Point
▼
If your ball rolls onto another court, wait for their point to finish — say "ball on" to warn them, but don't run on to grab it mid-rally. If you need to walk through another court to reach yours, wait for the point to end, ask politely, then move through quickly. Don't dilly-dally on their court.
